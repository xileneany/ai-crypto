Open source Apr 24
DeepSeek V4: frontier-class AI at 1/6th the price
V4-Pro: 1.6T parameters, 1M-token context, MIT licensed, $1.74/$3.48 per 1M tokens — 6× cheaper than Opus 4.7, 98% below GPT-5.5 Pro. Beats all open models on code benchmarks; trails GPT-5.4/Gemini-3.1-Pro by only 3–6 months. Crucially, trained partly on Huawei Ascend chips, bypassing US export restrictions. OpenAI launches GPT-5.5 — shadowed immediately by DeepSeek
GPT-5.5 rolls out to Plus/Pro/Business users with improved coding, deep research, and computer-use. At $5/$30 per 1M tokens, it's 2× more expensive than GPT-5.4. Labor Week
AI layoff wave: 100K+ tech jobs gone in 2026 to date
UKG shed 950 citing AI (Apr 21). Meta confirmed 8,000 layoffs (10% of staff) + 6,000 unfilled roles starting May 20, simultaneously doubling AI capex to $115–135B. Microsoft opened a "voluntary buyout" to ~8,750 US employees. WEF data cited by former UK PM Rishi Sunak: US entry-level job postings are down 35% in 18 months. Sunak told the BBC on Apr 22 and Apr 24 that graduates' fears about AI "flattening" entry-level hiring are "justified." Analysts project 300,000 tech job cuts by year-end. This is no longer a trend — it's structural.
05
Regulation 100 days
EU AI Act enforcement clock: 100 days to Aug 2 deadline
From Apr 25, companies have 100 days until the EU AI Act begins enforcing on "high-risk" AI systems used in recruitment, performance management, and workforce monitoring. Requires quality management systems, technical docs, and conformity records. The compliance burden quietly favors self-hosted models like DeepSeek V4 Flash — controlling the data path simplifies Article 10 (data governance) and Article 14 (human oversight) audits. Closed-API solutions face harder audit trails. A slow-burn story that's about to get very loud for enterprise legal teams.

Regulation May 3 ahead
CLARITY Act SEC roundtable confirmed for May 3
The SEC confirmed a formal CLARITY Act roundtable — the legislation that decides whether crypto assets are commodities (CFTC) or securities (SEC). Commodity framing = DeFi and tokenization green light. Securities framing = another 12+ months of ambiguity. Goldman Sachs survey: 35% of institutions cite regulatory uncertainty as the top barrier to adoption; 71% plan to increase crypto exposure in the next 12 months. The midterm election window means H1 2026 is the only viable passage timeline. Miss it and the bill slides past November.
04
Protocol Apr 15
BIP-361: Bitcoin proposes freezing $74B in quantum-vulnerable coins
Jameson Lopp and five developers published BIP-361 — a 3-phase plan to migrate Bitcoin to quantum-resistant cryptography. Phase B (5 years post-activation) renders legacy ECDSA/Schnorr signatures completely invalid. At stake: ~5.6M BTC that hasn't moved in 10+ years, plus Satoshi's estimated 1M BTC. Google's 2026 research suggests a future quantum machine could crack ECDSA in ~10 minutes. No such machine exists today — but Adam Back is already pushing back, proposing optional upgrades instead of forced freezes. The Bitcoin governance battle of the decade is opening.
05
Infrastructure Week
Stripe + Paradigm launch "Zones" — enterprise blockchain privacy layer
Zones allows parallel private blockchains to connect to a public mainnet for sensitive corporate tasks — payroll, fund management — keeping internal transaction details confidential while allowing cryptographic verification. This directly addresses the "confidentiality gap" that has historically blocked large-scale corporate blockchain adoption. Backed by both the largest global payments processor and the most influential crypto-native VC. This got buried under the ETF headlines but may prove to be the most structurally important story of the week.
Key trend

The great bifurcation

Compute kings vs. efficiency insurgents
This week crystallized the defining battle of 2026. On one side: Anthropic pulling in $65B in a week, Google and Amazon racing to lock up 5GW of compute, OpenAI building a $20B Cerebras deal — raw compute as competitive moat. On the other: DeepSeek V4 delivering near-frontier AI at 1/6th the cost by being architecturally smarter, trained on domestic chips, open-sourced under MIT, and priced below even small Western models.

The crypto parallel is exact. Bitcoin ETFs represent institutional capital capturing value through established rails — the compute kings play. DeepSeek is the decentralized insurgent that US export restrictions inadvertently created. The chip ban, designed to slow China, forced DeepSeek to build a more efficient architecture. It produced the opposite of its intended effect.

The EU AI Act clock adds a regulatory dimension: self-hosted open models like V4 Flash have compliance advantages that closed APIs can't easily match — you control the data path, which simplifies GDPR and Article 10/14 audit trails. The trillion-dollar question is whether raw compute dominance holds, or whether the efficiency insurgency commoditizes the frontier entirely — the same question crypto answered with Bitcoin in 2009.
